In this post we introduce the most typical line bundle (locally free sheaf) $\mathcal O(m)$ on projective space $\mathbb P^n_k$, give it a very concrete description, and prove the main theorem without invoking the theory of divisors.

For the projective space $\mathbb P^n_k$, we have $\operatorname{Pic}\mathbb P^n_k\simeq\mathbb Z$.

Along the way we describe the sections of $\mathcal O(m)$ and a few other important properties.

The sheaf $\mathcal O(1)$ on $\mathbb P^1$

Let us start with the simplest case, namely the definition of the sheaf $\mathcal O(1)$ on $\mathbb P^1$. Recall that projective space is the Proj construction $$\mathbb P^n_k:=\operatorname{Proj}k[x_0,x_1]$$ and admits two standard affine open subsets $$U_0=D_+(x_0)\simeq\operatorname{Spec}k[x_{1/0}],\quad U_1=D_+(x_1)\simeq\operatorname{Spec}k[x_{0/1}]$$ where $x_{i/j}$ denotes $x_i/x_j$ viewed as a coordinate. Write $t:=x_{1/0}$ and $u:=x_{0/1}$.

These two charts can be pictured as two affine lines glued together, as shown below:

On $U_0\cap U_1$ we always have $u=1/t$, that is, $$\begin{aligned} \Gamma(\mathcal O_{\mathbb P^1},U_0)&=k[t]\\ \Gamma(\mathcal O_{\mathbb P^1},U_1)=k[u]&=k[1/t]\\ \Gamma(\mathcal O_{\mathbb P^1},U_0\cap U_1)=k[t]\otimes_kk[1/t]&=k[t,1/t] \end{aligned}$$

With the setup in place, we define the line bundle $\mathcal O(1)$ by gluing two trivial line bundles on $\mathbb P^1$. On $U_0$ take a rank $1$ trivial $\mathcal O_{U_0}$-module $$\mathcal O_{U_0}e_0$$ and on $U_1$ a rank $1$ trivial $\mathcal O_{U_1}$-module $$\mathcal O_{U_1}e_1$$ We need to glue them on the overlap $U_0\cap U_1$, and we declare the gluing relation $$e_1=t\cdot e_0$$ Clearly $t=x_1/x_0$ is an invertible function on $U_0\cap U_1$, so this is a legitimate transition function. The resulting line bundle is denoted $\mathcal O(1)$. A section of this sheaf can be written as a pair $(f_0,f_1)\in k[t]\times k[u]$ satisfying $f_0e_0=f_1e_1$; since $e_1=t\cdot e_0$, we get $f_0=tf_1$, i.e. $f_0(t)=tf_1(1/t)$ in $k[t,1/t]$.

Let us compute the global sections of $\mathcal O(1)$. Write $$f_1(u)=a_0+a_1u+a_2u^2+\cdots+a_ru^r$$ Then $$\begin{aligned}tf_1(1/t)&=t(a_0+a_1t^{-1}+a_2t^{-2}+\cdots+a_rt^{-r})\\ &=a_0t+a_1+a_2t^{-1}+\cdots+a_rt^{1-r}\end{aligned}$$ For this to lie in $k[t]$, no negative powers of $t$ may appear, so necessarily $$a_2=a_3=\cdots=a_r=0$$ Hence $f_1(u)=a_0+a_1u$ and $f_0(t)=a_0t+a_1$, both linear functions. Since $k$ is a field, $$\Gamma(\mathbb P^1_k,\mathcal O(1))$$ is a vector space, and it has dimension $2$ since it is generated by the two sections $x_0$ and $x_1$. Explicitly, $$x_0\leftrightarrow(1,u),\quad x_1\leftrightarrow(t,1)$$ because on $U_0$, $x_0/x_0=1$ and on $U_1$, $x_0/x_1=1$; on $U_0$, $x_1/x_0=t$ and on $U_1$, $x_1/x_1=1$. Therefore $$\Gamma(\mathbb P^1_k,\mathcal O(1))= kx_0\oplus kx_1$$ This bundle is clearly nontrivial because $\dim_k\Gamma(\mathbb P^1_k,\mathcal O)=1$.

The sheaf $\mathcal O(m)$ on $\mathbb P^1$ for $m\ge 0$

Next we describe the slightly more general (but still elementary) sheaf $\mathcal O(m)$; the only change is that the transition function now carries a power. Again take $$\mathcal O_{U_0}e_0,\quad\mathcal O_{U_1}e_1$$ as two free rank-$1$ modules, and declare the gluing relation $$e_1=t^me_0$$ The resulting line bundle is $\mathcal O(m)$. A global section is a pair $(f_0(t),f_1(u))\in k[t]\times k[u]$ satisfying $$f_0(t)=t^mf_1(1/t)$$ To compute $\Gamma(\mathbb P^1_k,\mathcal O(m))$, write again $$f_1(u)=a_0+a_1u+a_2u^2+\cdots+a_ru^r$$ Then $$\begin{aligned}f_0(t)&=t^m(a_0+a_1t^{-1}+\cdots+a_rt^{-r})\\ &=a_0t^m+a_1t^{m-1}+\cdots+a_rt^{m-r}\end{aligned}$$ Negative powers must vanish, so we are left with $$f_1(u)=a_0+a_1u+a_2u^2+\cdots+a_mu^m,\quad f_0(t)=a_0t^m+a_1t^{m-1}+\cdots+a_m$$ all of degree $m$. The generators of $\Gamma(\mathbb P^1_k,\mathcal O(m))$ are $$x_0^m,x_0^{m-1}x_1,\ldots, x_1^m$$ corresponding to $$(1,u^m),(t,u^{m-1}),\ldots,(t^m,1)$$ Why? Simply divide by $x_0^m$ on $U_0$ and by $x_1^m$ on $U_1$. Hence $$\Gamma(\mathbb P^1,\mathcal O(m))\simeq\bigoplus_{i=0}^m{kx_0^ix_1^{m-i}}$$

The sheaf $\mathcal O(m)$ on $\mathbb P^1$ for $m<0$

Now consider the sheaf $\mathcal O(-m)$ on $\mathbb P^1_k$, say $\mathcal O(-1)$. The gluing relation becomes $$e_1=t^{-1}e_0$$ A global section is a pair $$(f_0(t),f_1(u))\in k[t]\times k[u]$$ satisfying $f_0(t)=t^{-1}f_1(1/t)$. Arguing as before, unless every coefficient of $f_1$ vanishes there will always be some negative power; hence $\Gamma(\mathbb P^1_k,\mathcal O(-1))=0$, and the same holds for any $-m$.

The sheaf $\mathcal O(m)$ on $\mathbb P^n$

Now consider $$\mathbb P^n_k:=\operatorname{Proj}k[x_0,\ldots,x_n]$$ whose standard opens are $U_i:=D_+(x_i)=\{x_i\ne 0\}$. On $U_i$ define $\mathcal O(m)|_{U_i}$ to be the trivial bundle $\mathcal O_{U_i}e_i$. On the overlap $U_i\cap U_j$ declare the gluing relation $$e_j=\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^me_i$$ The functions $g_{ij}=\left(\dfrac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^m$ are regular invertible functions on the overlap and satisfy the cocycle condition $$g_{ij}g_{jk}=g_{ik}$$ which is obvious because $$\left(\frac{x_{j}}{x_{i}}\right)^m\left(\frac{x_{k}}{x_{j}}\right)^m=\left(\frac{x_{k}}{x_{i}}\right)^m$$ They glue into a line bundle, namely $\mathcal O_{\mathbb P^n}(m)$. Similarly, writing a section as $f_ie_i$ on $U_i$ with $f_i\in\mathcal O(U_i)$, we have $f_ie_i=f_je_j$ on $U_i\cap U_j$, which immediately gives $$f_i=\left(\frac{x_{j}}{x_{i}}\right)^mf_j$$

Operations on the sheaves $\mathcal O(m)$

We have the following crucial result.

$\mathcal O$ is a group homomorphism $\mathbb Z\to\operatorname{Pic}\mathbb P^n_k$; that is, over a field $k$ and projective space $\mathbb P^n_k$ we have $$\mathcal O(a)\otimes_{\mathcal O_{\mathbb P^n}}\mathcal O(b)\simeq\mathcal O(a+b)$$ In particular, we will prove the following four facts (we omit the base of the tensor product):
  1. $\mathcal O(m)\simeq\mathcal O(1)^{\otimes m},\quad m>0$;
  2. $\mathcal O(-m)\simeq\mathcal O(-1)^{\otimes m},\quad m>0$;
  3. $\mathcal O(m)^\lor\simeq\mathcal O(-m)$;
  4. $\mathcal O(m)\otimes\mathcal O(-m)\simeq\mathcal O$.

We set up notation: for each $\mathcal O(m)$, on each standard open $U_i$ write the rank-$1$ $\mathcal O_{U_i}$-module basis as $e_i^{(m)}$. We have $$e_j^{(m)}=\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^me_i^{(m)}$$ and set $$g_{ij}^{(m)}=x_{i/j}^m=\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^m$$ which satisfies the cocycle condition.

(Proof of 1 and 2.) Let $\mathcal L=\mathcal O(1)^{\otimes m}$. On each $U_i$ the local basis of $\mathcal O(1)$ is $e_i^{(1)}$, so a local basis of $\mathcal L|_{U_i}$ may be taken to be $$f_i=\underbrace{e_i^{(1)}\otimes\cdots\otimes e_i^{(1)}}_{\#m}$$ The definition of $\mathcal O(1)$ gives $$e_i^{(1)}=\frac{x_j}{x_i}e_j^{(1)}$$ so on the overlap $U_i\cap U_j$ $$\begin{aligned} f_i&=\overbrace{e_i^{(1)}\otimes\cdots\otimes e_i^{(1)}}^{\#m}\\ &=\overbrace{\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}e_j^{(1)}\right)\otimes\cdots\otimes\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}e_j^{(1)}\right)}^{\#m}\\ &=\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^m\left(\overbrace{e_j^{(1)}\otimes\cdots\otimes e_j^{(1)}}^{\#m}\right)\\ &=\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^mf_j \end{aligned}$$ Hence the transition function of $\mathcal O(1)^{\otimes m}$ is $x_{j/i}^m$, which is precisely the transition function of $\mathcal O(m)$. The local isomorphisms $\phi_i:f_i\leftrightarrow e_i^{(m)}$ are immediate, and one checks easily that they are compatible on overlaps.

The proof of 2 is identical in form, only with different symbols.

(Proof of 3.) We know $$\mathcal O(m)^\lor=\mathscr{H\!\!om}_{\mathcal O_X}(\mathcal O(m),\mathcal O)$$ On $U_i$ the basis of $\mathcal O(m)$ is $e_i^{(m)}$, and we can clearly construct the dual basis $\left(e_i^{(m)}\right)^\lor$ satisfying $$\left(e_i^{(m)}\right)^\lor\left(e_i^{(m)}\right)=1$$ Now on the overlap $U_i\cap U_j$ consider $$e_i^{(m)}=\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^me_j^{(m)}$$ so $$e_j^{(m)}=\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^{-m}e_i^{(m)}$$ Set $$\left(e_i^{(m)}\right)^\lor=a_{ij}\left(e_j^{(m)}\right)^\lor$$ Applying both sides to $e_i^{(m)}$ gives $$1=\left(e_i^{(m)}\right)^\lor\left(e_i^{(m)}\right)=a_{ij}\left(e_j^{(m)}\right)^\lor\left(\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^me_j^{(m)}\right)$$ hence $$1=a_{ij}\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^m,\quad a_{ij}=\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^{-m}$$ So the transition function of $\mathcal O(m)^\lor$ is $\left(\dfrac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^{-m}$, the same as $\mathcal O(-m)$, giving the isomorphism.

(Proof of 4.) On $U_i$ consider the local basis of $\mathcal O(m)\otimes\mathcal O(-m)$, $$e_i^{(m)}\otimes e_i^{(-m)}$$ On the overlap $U_i\cap U_j$, $$e_i^{(m)}=\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^me_j^{(m)}$$ and $$e_i^{(-m)}=\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^{-m}e_j^{(-m)}$$ so $$\begin{aligned}e_i^{(m)}\otimes e_i^{(-m)}&=\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^me_j^{(m)}\otimes\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^{-m}e_j^{(-m)}\\ &=\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^m\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^{-m}\left(e_j^{(m)}\otimes e_j^{(-m)}\right)\\&=e_j^{(m)}\otimes e_j^{(-m)}\end{aligned}$$ Hence the transition function is $1$, the same as $\mathcal O$, giving the isomorphism analogously.

Proof of injectivity and surjectivity

We now prove Theorem 1 from the start of the article. It suffices to prove the two theorems below (injectivity and surjectivity); the proofs look long but consist entirely of elementary algebraic manipulations.

If $\mathcal O(m)\simeq\mathcal O(r)$, then $m=r$. Assuming $\mathcal O(m)\simeq\mathcal O(r)$, tensor with $\mathcal O(-r)$ to obtain $$\mathcal O(m-r)\simeq\mathcal O$$ The problem reduces to showing $\mathcal O(q)\simeq\mathcal O\implies q=0$. The transition function of $\mathcal O(q)$ is $$\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^q$$ If $\mathcal O(q)$ were trivial, this family of transition functions could be turned into the constant $1$ by a change of local basis. But on $U_i\simeq\mathbb A^n_k$ the only invertible regular functions are constants, $\Gamma(U_i,\mathcal O^\times_X)=k^\times$, so a change of basis can only modify the transition function to $$b_i\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^qb^{-1}_j=\frac{b_i}{b_j}\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^q$$ with $b_i,b_j\in k^\times$. A constant factor cannot cancel a nonzero power, so $\mathcal O(q)$ can be trivial only when $q=0$. Let $\mathcal L$ be an invertible sheaf on $\mathbb P^n_k$. Then there exists $m\in\mathbb Z$ such that $\mathcal L\simeq\mathcal O(m)$. Write $$X=\mathbb P^n_k,\quad U_i=D_+(x_i),\quad i=0,\ldots,n$$ so that $U_i\simeq\mathbb A^n_k$. Let $\mathcal L$ be an arbitrary invertible sheaf on $X$. Since every invertible sheaf on $\mathbb A^n_k$ is trivial, we may choose $\mathcal L|_{U_i}\simeq\mathcal O|_{U_i}$, i.e. pick a local basis on each $U_i$, $$e_i\in\Gamma(U_i,\mathcal L)$$ On the overlap $U_i\cap U_j$ the two bases $e_i,e_j$ are related by some regular invertible function $g_{ij}$: $$e_i=g_{ij}e_j$$ with $g_{ij}\in\Gamma(U_i\cap U_j,\mathcal O_X^\times)$. From $e_i=g_{ij}e_j$ and $e_j=g_{jk}e_k$ we get $e_i=g_{ij}g_{jk}e_k$, and on the other hand $e_i=g_{ik}e_k$, so $g_{ij}g_{jk}=g_{ik}$ (the cocycle condition). Thus an invertible sheaf is described by a family of invertible functions $\{g_{ij}\}$ satisfying $g_{ij}\in\Gamma(U_i\cap U_j,\mathcal O^\times_X)$ and $g_{ij}g_{jk}=g_{ik}$. Next, look at regular invertible functions on $U_i\cap U_j$. On $U_i\simeq\mathbb A^n_k$ we may use the coordinates $$\frac{x_0}{x_i},\ldots,\widehat{\frac{x_i}{x_i}},\ldots,\frac{x_n}{x_i}$$ and $$U_i\cap U_j=D\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)\subset U_i$$ so $$\Gamma(U_i\cap U_j,\mathcal O_X)\simeq k\left[\frac{x_0}{x_i},\ldots,\widehat{\frac{x_i}{x_i}},\ldots,\frac{x_n}{x_i},\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^{-1}\right]$$ This is a polynomial ring with one variable inverted; its units are exactly $$c\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^a,\quad c\in k^\times,\quad a\in\mathbb Z$$ Hence every transition function can be written uniquely as $$g_{ij}=c_{ij}\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^{a_{ij}},\quad c_{ij}\in k^\times,\quad a_{ij}\in\mathbb Z$$ Applying the cocycle condition gives $$c_{ij}c_{jk}\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^{a_{ij}}\left(\frac{x_k}{x_j}\right)^{a_{jk}}=c_{ik}\left(\frac{x_k}{x_i}\right)^{a_{ik}}\tag{I}$$ On the triple overlap $U_i\cap U_j\cap U_k$ set $$u=\frac{x_j}{x_i},\quad v=\frac{x_k}{x_i}$$ so that $\dfrac{x_k}{x_j}=\dfrac{v}{u}$. The monomial part of the left side of (I) becomes $$u^{a_{ij}}\left(\frac vu\right)^{a_{jk}}=u^{a_{ij}-a_{jk}}v^{a_{jk}}$$ and the right side is $$v^{a_{ik}}$$ Hence $a_{ij}-a_{jk}=0$ and $a_{jk}=a_{ik}$, so $a_{ij}=a_{jk}=a_{ik}$. All the $a_{ij}$ are equal to some common integer, call it $m$. Therefore $$g_{ij}=c_{ij}\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^m\tag{II}$$ In particular, for $n=1$ there are only two opens $U_0,U_1$ and no nonempty triple overlaps, so directly $$g_{01}\in\Gamma(U_0\cap U_1,\mathcal O^\times)=k^\times\left(\frac{x_1}{x_0}\right)^\mathbb Z$$ which still has the form $$g_{01}=c_{01}\left(\frac{x_1}{x_0}\right)^m$$ So (II) holds in every $n\ge 1$. It remains to handle the constants $c_{ij}\in k^\times$. The cocycle condition $c_{ij}c_{jk}=c_{ik}$ says $\{c_{ij}\}$ is itself a $1$-cocycle of constant functions. We kill it by a change of basis: set $$b_i=c_{0i}\in k^\times$$ Then $c_{0i}c_{ij}=c_{0j}$ gives $c_{ij}=\dfrac{c_{0j}}{c_{0i}}$. Replacing the local bases by $e'_i=b_ie_i$, the relation $e_i=g_{ij}e_j$ becomes $$e'_i=b_ie_i=b_ig_{ij}e_j=b_ig_{ij}b^{-1}_je'_j$$ so the new transition function is $g'_{ij}=b_ig_{ij}b^{-1}_j$. Plugging into (II), $$g'_{ij}=b_ic_{ij}b^{-1}_j\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^m$$ and $$b_ic_{ij}b^{-1}_j=c_{0i}\cdot\frac{c_{0j}}{c_{0i}}\cdot c^{-1}_{0j}=1$$ so $$g'_{ij}=\left(\frac{x_j}{x_i}\right)^m$$ which is the transition function of $\mathcal O(m)$. Q.E.D.
